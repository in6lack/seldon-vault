## The Problem

You open your news feed and see: a military buildup here, sanctions announced there, an election result somewhere else, a trade dispute escalating, a diplomatic summit producing mixed signals. Hundreds of articles daily, each with its own spin, its own narrative, its own editorial bias.

How do you figure out what actually matters?

Traditional approaches all have gaps. Reading everything is impossible. Following a single source gives you a single bias. Expert commentary is often contradictory — one analyst says escalation is imminent, another says it's all posturing. And most analysis is backward-looking: explaining what already happened, not what's likely to happen next.

## What Seldon Vault Offers

### Noise Filtering

From hundreds of daily signals across 30+ data sources (GDACS, ACLED, SIPRI, government feeds, global media), Seldon Vault distills 3-5 key forecasts daily. Each one is a specific, falsifiable claim with a probability attached. Not "tensions are rising" — but "escalation to direct military confrontation: 34% probability."

### Multiple Perspectives

Seven specialist analysts examine every significant event from different angles:

- **Geopolitician** — alliances, sovereignty, diplomatic dynamics
- **Economist** — trade flows, sanctions impact, financial stress
- **Military Analyst** — force posture, logistics, escalation ladders
- **Cybersecurity Analyst** — digital warfare, APT activity, infrastructure threats
- **Sociopolitical Analyst** — public opinion, protest dynamics, social cohesion
- **Climatologist** — resource scarcity, climate migration, environmental stress
- **Technology Analyst** — emerging tech impact, innovation disruption, digital transformation

### Probabilistic Framing

Every forecast comes with a probability, not a certainty. "72% chance of sanctions expansion" is more useful than "sanctions are likely" because it communicates both the direction AND the confidence level. When the situation changes, the probability updates — you can literally watch it move.

### Cascade Narratives

Events don't happen in isolation. Seldon Vault traces the chains: conflict leads to sanctions, sanctions lead to economic pressure, economic pressure leads to social unrest, social unrest leads to political instability. These Cascade Narratives show you the second- and third-order effects that most analysis misses.

### Interactive World Map

A visual overview of global risk levels by region, updated daily. See at a glance where tensions are highest, which regions are trending up or down, and where Seldon Crises have been declared.

### Bayesian Updates

Watch how probabilities evolve as situations develop. A forecast might start at 40%, jump to 65% after a military deployment, then drop to 50% after a diplomatic meeting. The update history shows you exactly what evidence drove each change.

## Example Workflow

You hear about rising tensions between two countries. Instead of reading 50 articles with conflicting takes, you check Seldon Vault:

1. **Find the forecast** — search by region or keyword on seldonvault.io
2. **See the probability** — "Armed conflict within 90 days: 28%"
3. **Read agent analyses** — each specialist explains what they see from their domain
4. **Check the Skeptic's review** — what's the case AGAINST escalation?
5. **Follow the Cascade Narrative** — if conflict happens, what are the downstream effects?
6. **Monitor updates** — subscribe via SSE or check back daily to see how the probability evolves

This takes five minutes instead of two hours, and gives you a structured, multi-perspective view instead of one journalist's opinion.

---

## Limitations

- **Seldon Vault doesn't replace deep expertise** in a specific region. A scholar who has spent 20 years studying a particular country will have insights that no AI system can match.
- **Daily updates, not minute-by-minute breaking news.** The analysis pipeline runs daily with 6-hourly Bayesian updates. For breaking news, you still need traditional news sources.
- **AI analysis may miss cultural nuances** that local experts catch. Language, historical memory, and social dynamics sometimes matter more than the data suggests.
